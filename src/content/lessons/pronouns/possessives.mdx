# Possessives

Possessive pronouns are classified into two types according to their function in the sentence.

## 1. Possessive Adjectives

Possessive adjectives show _who owns something_. They are placed _before a noun_ and cannot be used alone.

<section className='flex flex-col gap-4 lg:flex-row'>

| Word      | Meaning                | Example                  |
| --------- | ---------------------- | ------------------------ |
| **my**    | mi / mis               | This is my house.        |
| **your**  | tu / tus               | This is your book.       |
| **his**   | su / sus (de él)       | This is his bicycle.     |
| **her**   | su / sus (de ella)     | This is her dress        |
| **its**   | su / sus (de eso)      | What is its name?        |
| **our**   | nuestro/a/os/as        | These are our suitcases. |
| **your**  | vuestro/a/os/as        | These are your seats.    |
| **their** | su / sus (de ellos/as) | These are their books.   |

    <div className='flex flex-col gap-4'>
        <LessonBlock variant='structure'>
            - Possessive adjective + noun
        </LessonBlock>

        <LessonBlock variant='important'>
            Possessive adjectives must always be followed by a noun.
        </LessonBlock>
    </div>

</section>

## 2. Possessive Pronouns

Possessive pronouns replace a noun phrase to avoid repetition. Unlike possessive adjectives, they are _not followed by a noun_.

<section className='flex flex-col gap-4 lg:flex-row'>

| Word       | Meaning                     | Example                |
| ---------- | --------------------------- | ---------------------- |
| **mine**   | mío / mía / míos / mías     | This book is mine.     |
| **yours**  | tuyo / tuya / tuyos / tuyas | Is this book yours?    |
| **his**    | suyo (de él)                | This bicycle is his.   |
| **hers**   | suyo / suya (de ella)       | The dress is hers.     |
| **ours**   | nuestro/a/os/as             | This house is ours.    |
| **yours**  | vuestro/a/os/as             | These seats are yours. |
| **theirs** | suyo/a/os/as (de ellos/as)  | This pencil is theirs. |

    <div className='flex flex-col gap-4'>
        <LessonBlock variant='structure'>
            - Subject + verb + possessive pronoun
        </LessonBlock>

        <LessonBlock variant='important'>
            Possessive pronouns replace a noun and are never followed by another noun.
        </LessonBlock>
    </div>

</section>

## 3. Possessive Adjectives vs. the Genitive Case

Both possessive adjectives and the genitive case (**'s**) express possession, but they are used differently.

<LessonBlock variant='example'>

- Her dress. → (Su vestido - de ella)
- Andrea's dress. → (El vestido de Andrea)

<p className='py-2'>In both examples, we have:</p>
<div className='ml-4'>

- A **possessor** (the owner)
- A **possession** (the thing that belongs to the owner)

</div>

</LessonBlock>

### Difference

- In _"her dress"_, the possessive adjective **her** is used because the owner is already known or understood from the context.
- In _"Andrea's dress"_, the owner's name is explicitly mentioned.

### Compare

| Possessive Adjective | Genitive Case         |
| -------------------- | --------------------- |
| her dress            | Andrea's dress        |
| his car              | John's car            |
| their house          | Maria and Tom's house |
| our teacher          | Sarah's teacher       |

### Rule

Use a _possessive adjective_ when the owner is represented by a pronoun:

- my book
- your car
- her dress

Use the **genitive case ('s)** when the owner is a specific person or noun:

- Andrea's dress
- John's car
- Sarah's teacher

<LessonBlock variant='important'>
  Both forms show possession. The difference is whether the owner is expressed with a pronoun (**my, your, his, her,
  our, their**) or with a noun (**Andrea, John, Sarah**).
</LessonBlock>

## 4. Grammatical Rules

<section className='grid grid-cols-1 lg:grid-cols-2 gap-4'>

<GrammarPoint title='1. Use the Genitive (’s) with People'>
    We normally use **'s** to show possession when the owner is a **person**.
    
    <LessonBlock variant='example'>
        - Paul's house.
        - Mary's bike.
        - John's car.
        - Sarah's book.
    </LessonBlock>

    The possessor comes first, followed by **'s** and the possessed noun.

</GrammarPoint>

<GrammarPoint title='2. Use **of** with Things and Places'>
    When referring to **things, objects, or places**, we usually use the preposition **of**.
    
    <LessonBlock variant='example'>
        - The wheel of the bike.
        - The roof of the house.
        - Washington is the capital of the United States.
    </LessonBlock>

    This structure is more common when the owner is not a person.

</GrammarPoint>

<GrammarPoint title='3. Omit the Noun When It Is Already Understood' className='col-span-2'>
    The possessed noun can be omitted when it is clear from the context.
    
    <section className='flex flex-col gap-4 lg:flex-row'>
        <LessonBlock variant='example'>
            - Question: Where is your sister?
            - Answer: She is at my parents'. _(my parents' house)_
        </LessonBlock>

        <LessonBlock variant='example'>
            - Question: Whose house is this?
            - Answer: It is Michael's. _(Michael's house)_
        </LessonBlock>
    </section>

    The noun is omitted because it is already understood by the listener.

</GrammarPoint>

<GrammarPoint title='4. Plural Possessors' className='col-span-2'>
    When a plural noun already ends in **-s**, add only an apostrophe (**'**) after the **s**.
    
    <section className='flex flex-col gap-4 lg:flex-row'>
        <LessonBlock variant='example'>
            - The girls' toys.
            - The students' exams.
            - The teachers' room.
        </LessonBlock>

        | Singular Possessor | Plural Possessor    |
        | ------------------ | ------------------- |
        | The girl's toy     | The girls' toys     |
        | The student's book | The students' books |
        | The teacher's room | The teachers' room  |

        </section>

    Singular nouns take **'s**, while plural nouns ending in **-s** take only **'**.

</GrammarPoint>

</section>
