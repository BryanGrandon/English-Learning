# There Be

The structure **_there + be_** is used to say that _something exists or is present_.

In Spanish, it usually means **"hay"**.

<Callout type="important">

**There** does **not** refer to a place in this structure. It is a **dummy subject** used only to introduce the existence of something.

- There is a book on the table.
- There are two cars outside.

</Callout>

The verb **be** changes depending on whether the noun is **singular** or **plural**.

- **There is** → singular nouns and uncountable nouns.
- **There are** → plural nouns.

---

## There Is

<Grammar type="rule" title="General Rule">

- #### Use **_there is_** with:
  - singular countable nouns
  - uncountable nouns

</Grammar>

<LessonGrid className='md:grid-cols-2'>

<Grammar 
  type="structure" 
  title="Singular Countable Noun" 
  highlight="There is + singular noun"
>

- #### Examples
  - There is a book on the desk.
  - There is an apple in the basket.

</Grammar>

<Grammar 
  type="structure" 
  title="Uncountable Noun" 
  highlight="There is + uncountable noun"
>

- #### Examples
  - There is some water in the bottle.
  - There is a lot of traffic today.

</Grammar>

</LessonGrid>

### Short Form

The contracted form of **there is** is **_there's_**.

<Callout type="example_sentences">

- There's a cat in the garden.
- There's some milk in the fridge.

</Callout>

---

## There Are

<Grammar type="rule" title="General Rule">

- #### Use **there are** with:
  - plural countable nouns

</Grammar>

<Grammar type="structure" title="Plural Noun" highlight="There are + plural noun">

- #### Examples
  - There are two books on the desk.
  - There are three apples in the basket.
  - There are many students in the classroom.
  - There are five chairs in the room.

</Grammar>

<Callout type="note">

Unlike **there is**, **there are** is **_not usually contracted_**. Native speakers normally say **there are** instead of **_there're_**.

</Callout>

---

## Sentence Structure

The structure of **_there + be_** changes depending on the type of sentence: **affirmative**, **negative**, or **interrogative**.

<LessonGrid className='md:grid-cols-2'>
<Grammar type="structure" title="Affirmative" highlight="There is/are + noun">

- #### Examples
  - There is a book on the table.
  - There are three cats in the house.

</Grammar>

<Grammar type="structure" title="Negative" highlight="There is/are + not + noun">

- #### Examples
  - There isn't a problem.
  - There aren't any apples.

</Grammar>

<Grammar type="structure" title="Interrogative" highlight="Is/Are + there + noun?">

- #### Examples
  - Is there a bank near here?
  - Are there any restaurants?

</Grammar>
</LessonGrid>

---

## There vs It

**There** and **it** are both used to talk about things, but they have different purposes.

<LessonGrid className='md:grid-cols-2'>

<Grammar type="comparison" title="There" highlight='There introduces something new or shows that something exists.'>

- #### Examples
  - There is a book on the table.
  - There is a problem with the computer.

</Grammar>

<Grammar type="comparison" title="It" highlight='It refers to something already known or mentioned.'>

- #### Examples
  - There is a book. **It** is new.
  - There is a new student. **It** is interesting.

</Grammar>

</LessonGrid>

<Callout type="note">

In **_there + be_**, the word **there** does not indicate a place. It introduces the existence of something.

</Callout>
