# Cardinal Numbers

Cardinal numbers are used to _count people and things_ and **express quantity**.

<LessonBlock icon='example' title='Examples' container="section" heading="p" >
  - one.
  - two.
  - three.
  - ten.
  - twenty.
  - one hundred.
</LessonBlock>

<LessonBlock title='Used For' icon="usage" container="blockquote" heading="p">

  - Dates.
  - Prices.
  - Quantities.

## Basic Numbers (1–12)

These are the first twelve numbers in English. They form the basis for counting, telling time, dates, ages, and larger numbers.

<LessonBlock icon='note' title='Remember' container='aside' heading='p'>
  Numbers 1–12 are the base for all larger numbers in English.
</LessonBlock>

## Numbers 13–19

The numbers 13 through 19 follow a simple pattern. They all end in “-teen,” which represents the numbers 13 through 19.

<LessonColumns className='sm:grid-cols-2'>
  <LessonBlock icon='rule' title='Rule' container='aside' heading='p'>
    - They end in **-teen**
  </LessonBlock>
</LessonColumns>

## Tens (20–90)

The numbers from 20 to 90 are the multiples of ten in English. Learning them will help you form larger numbers, such as twenty-one, thirty-five, and ninety-nine.

<LessonColumns className='sm:grid-cols-2'>
  <LessonBlock icon='rule' title='Rule' container='aside' heading='p'>
    - They end in **-ty**
  </LessonBlock>
</LessonColumns>

<GroupVocabularyCard values={TENS_NUMBERS} />

## Compound Numbers (21–99)

The numbers from 21 to 99 are formed by combining a multiple of ten with a single-digit number.

<LessonColumns className='sm:grid-cols-2'>
  <LessonBlock icon='rule' title='Rule' container='aside' heading='p'>
    - tens + **_"-"_** + number
  </LessonBlock>
  <LessonBlock icon='example' title='Example' container='section' heading='p'>
    - twenty-one → 21
  </LessonBlock>
  <LessonBlock icon='example' title='Example Sentence' container='section' heading='p'>
    - I am **twenty-one** years old.
  </LessonBlock>
</LessonColumns>

## Hundreds, Thousands, Millions

Learn how to express larger numbers in English by combining hundreds, thousands, and millions.

<LessonColumns className='sm:grid-cols-2 lg:grid-cols-3'>
  <LessonBlock title='Hundreds' icon='note' heading='h3' variant='default' container='section'>

    - one hundred → 100
    - two hundred → 200
    - five hundred → 500

  </LessonBlock>
  <LessonBlock title='Thousands' icon='note' heading='h3' variant='default' container='section'>

    - one thousand → 1,000
    - ten thousand → 10,000
    - fifty thousand → 50,000

  </LessonBlock>
  <LessonBlock title='Millions' icon='note' heading='h3' variant='default' container='section'>

    - one million → 1,000,000
    - two million → 2,000,000
    - ten million → 10,000,000

  </LessonBlock>
</LessonColumns>

<LessonBlock title='Remember' icon="concept" heading='p' variant='default' container='section'>
  Hundred, thousand, and million do not take -s when they follow a number.

- ✅ two hundred
- ✅ five thousand
- ✅ three million

</LessonBlock>

### Using **_"and"_**

- #### In British English, and is commonly used between the hundreds and the tens or units.
  - one hundred and twenty → 120
  - three hundred and fifty-five → 355
  - one thousand two hundred and ten → 1,210

- #### American English often omits and in whole numbers:
  - one hundred twenty → 120
  - three hundred fifty-five → 355
